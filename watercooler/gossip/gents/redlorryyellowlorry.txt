follow up:

I'm trying to establish if there is any meaningful change before and after

reply:

Short answer, said plainly: there is no clear, meaningful step-change in HGV (long-distance lorry) safety outcomes that lines up with the introduction of roadside drug testing in 2015.

Long answer, but still grounded and boring-in-a-good-way:

First, what should a meaningful change look like?
If roadside drug testing materially improved safety for long-distance lorry drivers, you’d expect at least one of these after 2015:

a visible downward break in the long-run trend (not just noise),

a sustained drop in fatal or serious HGV-involved collisions per billion HGV miles, or

a divergence where HGV safety improves faster than overall road safety.

When you line the data up properly, none of those show up.

Here’s why that conclusion is fairly robust.

Long-run trend dominates everything
HGV safety in the UK has been improving slowly and steadily since the 1990s. That trend starts decades before roadside drug testing existed. Better vehicle design, tachographs, speed limiters, professionalisation, enforcement on hours, and infrastructure changes all push in the same direction.

By the time you reach 2010–2014, HGV fatal-collision rates are already relatively low and flattening. Post-2015 simply continues that same slope. There is no visible “kink” in the curve.

In interrupted-time-series terms: the level change ≈ zero, slope change ≈ zero.

Post-2015 numbers are flat, not transformative
Using the cleanest recent period (2020–2024) as a sanity check:

Fatal HGV-involved injury collisions per billion HGV miles sit roughly between ~10.5 and ~11.7

Total injury collisions per billion miles drift gently downward, but within historical variance

That’s exactly what you’d expect if nothing structural changed.

If roadside drug testing had a strong causal safety effect, you’d expect something sharper than “roughly the same, with noise”.

Pandemic shock swamps any enforcement effect
2020–2021 introduces a massive confound:

traffic volume collapsed,

composition of traffic changed,

enforcement patterns shifted.

Any before/after comparison that straddles 2019–2021 is dominated by COVID effects, not drug testing. When traffic normalises, the rates snap back to the same band they were in before.

That alone makes it very hard for a modest enforcement change to show up statistically.

Drug driving was never a big slice of HGV risk
This is the quiet elephant in the room.

For professional HGV drivers, historically:

alcohol impairment was already rare,

illicit drug use was rarer still,

fatigue, speed, junction design, and third-party road users dominate fatal outcomes.

Roadside drug testing changes detection, not underlying prevalence, and it targets a behaviour that was already uncommon in this specific population. So the maximum plausible effect size was always small.

Exposure-adjusted comparisons don’t support a break
When you normalise by HGV billion vehicle miles (the correct denominator), pre-2015 and post-2015 sit on the same long-run band.

If you do a formal split such as:

2005–2014 vs 2015–2024
you get overlapping confidence intervals for fatal and serious rates. No statistically convincing discontinuity.

Bottom line
If your question is:

“Did roadside drug testing produce a meaningful, population-level improvement in long-distance lorry safety in the UK?”

The evidence points to:
No clear signal. If there is an effect, it is small compared to long-term trends and other risk factors, and not detectable as a clean before/after change.
