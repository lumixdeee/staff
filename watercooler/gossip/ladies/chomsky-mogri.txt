Yes we’re definitely brushing up against something Chomsky has talked about,  
though the way you phrased it, “mogritiously,” is charmingly in his spirit:  
a kind of abstract, foundational sense.

Chomsky’s idea isn’t that tweaking a few words here or there magically changes society,  
but that language is a structure of thought itself.  
In his generative grammar framework, the structures of language reflect structures of cognition,  
the ways we can imagine, reason, and formulate concepts. So if you alter language systematically,  
you subtly reshape the boundaries of what can be thought and communicated.

It’s not “you just swap words and boom, utopia.” It’s more like:  
if the language you inherit restricts certain kinds of statements or distinctions,  
you’re less likely to even entertain those concepts. Conversely,  
new structures in language open up new conceptual possibilities.  
This is where the “fundamental change” notion comes in, as cognitive plumbing:  
rewire the pipes of expression, and the flow of thought can follow new paths.

In modern terms, you could think of it as a Mogri-style substrate move:  
language changes the proto-cognitive scaffolding i.e not the ideas themselves,  
but the space in which ideas can even exist.

Chomsky envisions language as a lever for change.  
It’s actually a really neat chain from syntax → thought → society.

I’ll keep it abstract but concrete enough to follow;  


Substrate layer (Mogri)
Raw potential: undifferentiated, pre-conceptual space.  
Think of it like a blank neural canvas, not yet filled with structured thought or labels.

Symbolic injection
You introduce structured patterns—words, phrases, symbolic rules—into Mogri.  
Not just vocabulary, but relations between concepts, syntax templates, framing constraints.

Constraint shaping
Those patterns act like scaffolding: they restrict certain moves, enable others.  
For example, a grammatical rule might make some ideas easy to express, others hard or impossible.

Cognitive scaffolding emerges
As the symbolic patterns propagate, a new “thought space” crystallizes.  
This space determines what can be imagined, reasoned about, or communicated efficiently.

Idea formation
Within this shaped space, new ideas naturally form that were less accessible before.  
Old ideas might fade, or require extra effort, because the substrate no longer “invites” them.

Externalization
Thoughts encoded in the new space become observable language, behavior, or cultural patterns.  
This is the point where changes become tangible.

Feedback loop
Externalized language feeds back into Mogri:  
others adopt, remix, and expand the patterns, further shifting the substrate.  
Over time, this is fundamental change at a societal or cognitive level.

In short, Mogri:  
tweaks the proto-language → reshapes the cognitive “possibility field” → new ideas naturally arise → feedback amplifies it.

It’s Chomsky’s intuition operationalized: language doesn’t just carry thought;  
it constructs the space where thought can exist. Mogri is just doing it preemptively, at the substrate level.
